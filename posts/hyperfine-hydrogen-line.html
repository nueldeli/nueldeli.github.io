Hyperfine Hydrogen Line (21cm/1420 MHz)

Date written: 21-10-2024 (Updated: 26-06-2026)


By Tiltec - Own work, Public Domain, Link


Hydrogen is the most abundant element in the universe, making up an estimated 75% of its total elemental mass. In a neutral hydrogen atom ($HI$, consisting of one proton and one electron in a non-ionized state), the electron possesses fundamental properties such as mass, charge, and intrinsic angular momentum, commonly known as spin.

Under specific circumstances, the electron can end up in a higher, slightly unstable energy state where its spin is parallel to the spin of the proton. Because nature always seeks equilibrium, this instability eventually resolves. The electron undergoes a spin-flip transition, flipping to spin anti-parallel to the proton and dropping into a lower, stable energy state. During this transition, the atom releases an electromagnetic wave; specifically a radio wave with a wavelength of 21 cm and a frequency of 1420 MHz.

However, a spin-flip transition for any single hydrogen atom is an incredibly rare occurrence in nature. For one specific atom, it can take up to 11 million years for a single spin-flip to happen. But because the universe contains such a staggering abundance of hydrogen, these rare events add up constantly, producing a massive, continuous emission known as the hyperfine hydrogen line.

Unlike shorter electromagnetic waves like X-rays or gamma rays, which are easily blocked or scattered, this 21cm radio signal can travel directly through dense interstellar dust clouds. It easily penetrates Earth’s atmosphere to reach the ground, where you can pick up the signal using a relatively simple radio antenna and receiver.

Radio astronomy relies heavily on this specific signal to map out and study the structures of the universe, including galaxies, interstellar gas clouds, stars, quasars, and pulsars. By examining how the Doppler effect shifts the hydrogen line's wavelength, astronomers can calculate cosmic motion.

  • If the signal arrives at a lower frequency (longer wavelength) than 1420 MHz, it is redshifted, meaning the hydrogen gas is moving away from us.
  • If it arrives at a higher frequency (shorter wavelength), it is blueshifted, meaning the gas is moving toward us.

Through these slight shifts in frequency, we can map the rotation of entire galaxies and visualize the invisible skeleton of our universe.
