Hyperfine Hydrogen Line (21cm/1420 MHz)

Date written: 21-10-2024

The hydrogen is the most abundant element in the universe and estimated to make up an estimated 75% of its mass. For a neutral hydrogen (HI one proton and one electron, non-ionized), it is known that an electron has its property such as mass, charge and angular momentum (spin).

In a specific circumstance, this electron might be in a higher energy state when it’s spinning parallel to the spin of the proton and it’s unstable. This instability will bound to find equilibrium and the electron will experience a spin-flip transition where it spins anti-parallel to the proton and fall in to a lower energy state. In this process, it releases an electromagnetic wave, in this case, in the form of radio wave which has the wavelength of 21 cm and the frequency of 1420Mhz.

However, an electron having a spin-flip transition is a rare occurrence in nature. For one hydrogen atom, it may take up to 11 million years for a spin to happen. But since the universe has abundant amount of hydrogen, this rarity added up to a point there’s a huge amount of hyperfine hydrogen line.

Unlike some electromagnetic wave such as gamma ray and X-ray, this radio signal can travel across interstellar clouds and penetrates Earth’s atmosphere and reach to the ground and you can pick the signal using an antenna or a receiver.

Radio astronomy utilizes this radio signal to study the properties of the universe such as galaxy, interstellar clouds, stars, quasars and pulsars. By examining the Doppler effect that affects the hydrogen line wavelength, astronomer can map out those interstellar activities and properties. When it has a lower frequency (long wavelength) than 1420Mhz, it is known to be redshifted, meaning the object is moving away from us. If it has a higher frequency (shorter wavelength), it means the signal is blue-shifted which means the object is moving towards us.
