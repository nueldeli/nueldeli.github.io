Hydrogen Atom Nomenclature

Date written: 22-10-2024

The hydrogen atom can exist in different states depending on its ionization state. In the typical nomenclature, hydrogen atom can exist in HI, HII and as molecules. This is specific to the hydrogen atom because it has one fixed number of electron. But for other element such as oxygen (O) it can exist in the state of OI, OII, and OIII.

For hydrogen atom, HI refers to the neutral hydrogen which means the atom does not ionized and it is in original state. During the spin-flip transition, this neutral atom will release a 1420MHz radio signal frequency.

HII means that the atom is losing one electron, making the atom ionized to be positively charged. In the outer space, the electron of the hydrogen atom that are exposed to a high energy radiation will cause the electron to stripped off from its atom. Such high-energy radiation typically causes by the young stars or any other stars.

H2 is a typical molecular hydrogen. It exists as molecule which 2 neutral hydrogen atoms are bound together. This type of hydrogen molecule is usually found in the cold and dense region of space such as molecular clouds.

In essence, the Roman numerals corresponds to the number of electrons the atom has lost:

  • I - neutral (no electron lost)
  • II - ionized (one electron lost)
  • III - doubly ionized (two electrons lost)
