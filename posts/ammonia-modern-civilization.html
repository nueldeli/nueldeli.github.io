Ammonia and the Modern Civilization

Date written: 15-04-2023

Ammonia is often thought of as the sharp-smelling ingredient in cleaning products, but this gas has profoundly changed human history. Its impact spans from ancient fertilizer techniques to a 20th-century revolution in food production. Today, ammonia plays a central role in agriculture, industry, and our ability to sustain a modern, growing world.

Long before we had modern science, people understood the importance of fertilizing the soil to grow healthy crops. Ancient civilizations, including the Romans and Egyptians, used organic sources like animal manure to enrich their fields. But this approach only provided a limited supply of nutrients, meaning crop yields were constrained.

The Haber-Bosch Process

The real breakthrough came in the early 1900s. Scientists knew nitrogen was essential for plants, but nitrogen in the air wasn’t usable by crops. German chemist Fritz Haber discovered a method to "fix" nitrogen, combining it with hydrogen to create ammonia. Later, Carl Bosch, an engineer, scaled up this method for mass production in what became known as the Haber-Bosch process. This process allowed humanity to produce ammonia in large quantities for the first time, creating a reliable source of nitrogen for fertilizers.

This development couldn't have come at a better time. At the start of the 20th century, the global population was booming, and there was concern that we wouldn’t be able to produce enough food to feed everyone. The Haber-Bosch process changed everything. With ammonia-based fertilizers, farmers could boost crop yields dramatically, supporting more people on less land. This was one of the most significant scientific achievements in history, laying the foundation for the "Green Revolution" of the mid-20th century, which saw global food production soar.



What If the Haber-Bosch Process Had Never Existed?

  • Global Hunger and Malnutrition: Before the Haber-Bosch process, natural fertilizers like manure were the only way to replenish soil nutrients. Without ammonia-based fertilizers, the world’s agricultural capacity would be severely limited. This would mean significantly lower crop yields, making it much harder to feed the current global population, which is over 8 billion people. Food shortages and malnutrition would likely be widespread, and hunger would be an even greater global crisis.
  • Stalled Population Growth and Urbanization: The boost in food production from ammonia fertilizers allowed for rapid population growth in the 20th century. Without it, population growth would likely have been much slower, and rural farming communities would dominate society. Urbanization, which relies on a surplus of food from farms, would be limited, meaning fewer cities and smaller economies worldwide.
  • Delayed Technological and Economic Development: Fewer people moving to cities would mean less industrial and technological growth, as a larger portion of the population would be required to work on farms to produce enough food. This could slow down innovation in fields ranging from medicine to transportation to technology, meaning we might be living in a less advanced world today.
  • Geopolitical Implications: Nations have often relied on food security as a source of stability. The inability to produce enough food would make many countries more vulnerable to economic collapse or political unrest. Moreover, ammonia’s role in explosives would mean limited military capabilities, which could have shifted the balance of power during conflicts like the World Wars.
  • Environmental and Land Use Consequences: Without high-yield fertilizers, farmers would need far more land to produce food. This would likely result in widespread deforestation and loss of natural habitats to make space for farms. Ironically, this could lead to even greater environmental harm than what we see today from ammonia’s production-related emissions.

In Modern Days

Today, around 80% of ammonia produced globally is used for fertilizers. The impact has been nothing short of revolutionary: ammonia fertilizers allowed farms to sustain larger harvests, and in turn, fueled rapid urbanization as fewer people were needed in agricultural roles. This shift allowed for economic growth and enabled more people to move to cities, fueling industries beyond farming.

Ammonia’s role wasn’t limited to food. As industrialization took off, ammonia found its way into a range of other uses. During both World Wars, ammonia was a critical ingredient for manufacturing explosives, including TNT, which relied on fixed nitrogen. This dual role of ammonia—as a tool for food production and military power—gave it a unique position in modern history.

In peacetime, ammonia also proved invaluable in manufacturing materials we use every day. Ammonia is used to produce nylon, found in everything from clothing to toothbrush bristles, and in plastics and synthetic fibers that power the textile and automotive industries. In a way, ammonia doesn’t just feed our bodies; it’s woven into the fabric of modern society.

Yet, ammonia’s widespread use has brought challenges. Producing ammonia is highly energy-intensive, typically requiring fossil fuels, and contributes significantly to greenhouse gas emissions. Additionally, ammonia can be harmful if not managed carefully; it’s toxic in high concentrations and can cause water pollution when runoff from fertilized fields reaches rivers and lakes.

Vaclav Smil, an expert on energy and agriculture, has highlighted the environmental costs of the Haber-Bosch process. In his book Enriching the Earth: Fritz Haber, Carl Bosch, and the Transformation of World Food Production, Smil explains that while ammonia-based fertilizers have lifted billions out of food insecurity, they also contribute to climate change. Smil argues that to balance ammonia’s benefits with environmental needs, we must develop more sustainable production methods in the future.
