The Art of War - Sun Tzu

Date written: 07-09-2025 (Updated: 26-06-2026)

I finally managed to get my hands on Sun Tzu's The Art of War after hearing about it for the past few years. I kept wondering why people still talk about it, despite the ancient text being written over 2,500 years ago. It turns out the philosophy behind the book is still applicable today, especially when it comes to running an organization. I read the Collins Classics edition; a neat little volume of only 96 pages, including the glossary

Sun Tzu was a military general who served King Helu of the Wu State from 514 to 496 BC. What I find fascinating is that he viewed and approached war as an art. These days, however, most people study his ideas as the "Science of War." It is a deeply thoughtful work on how to win campaigns and defeat enemies. Reading the English translation, I appreciated how his ideas were laid out in a clear, concise, and easy-to-digest format.

"All war is based on deception".

The first thing that came to my mind when reading this was the movie Argo (2012). The film is based on the real-life operation to rescue six American diplomats during the 1979 Iran Hostage Crisis. The CIA fabricated a cover story, making it look like the diplomats were part of a Canadian film crew scouting locations for a science fiction film called Argo. They forged documents, prepared props, and even brought in Hollywood insiders to legitimize the ruse—all to deceive the Iranian authorities. Thanks to this elaborate deception, the diplomats managed to escape safely.

"If you know yourself and your enemy then you can always win. If you know only yourself, then you may win or lose. If you know neither yourself or your enemy, then you most likely will lose."

This foundational quote essentially captures the core premise of the book. It is vital to know your own strengths and weaknesses, but equally important to understand those of your enemy and the physical environment where the battle will be fought.

What captured my attention the most, however, was Sun Tzu's heavy emphasis on the supply chain. He stresses that war is not won solely by battlefield strategy, but by ensuring the army is well-fed and its supplies remain intact. In warfare, speed matters; the longer a campaign drags on, the more it drains resources. He also introduces the concept of leveraging local resources: using the enemy's supplies to reduce the burden on your own logistics.

This reminded me of the United States military, which is widely recognized for having the most robust and advanced logistical capability in the world. Logistics is strategy. During Operation Desert Storm in 1991, the US deployed around half a million troops and tons of equipment to the Middle East in a matter of months. Conversely, it also brought to mind Operation Barbarossa in 1941. Severe supply chain failures halted the German army's advancement into Soviet territory. Their supply lines stretched for hundreds of miles, and because they relied primarily on horse-and-wagon transportation, their logistics were wholly insufficient for modern warfare.

My favorite chapters are Chapter 9 (The Army on the March), Chapter 10 (Terrain), and Chapter 11 (The Nine Situations), which gave me an entirely new perspective on the battlefield. When people talk about war, the most common aspects mentioned are strategy and sheer strength, often without understanding the underlying nuances.

In Chapter 9, Sun Tzu highlights outsmarting the enemy through close observation—learning to read environmental cues like scattered birds, dust clouds, and campfires. In daily life, we are taught to "read the room," but in war, this skill determines whether men live or die.

In Chapter 10, he emphasizes how terrain shapes an enemy’s choices, advising leaders to pick ground where the enemy is disadvantaged and constrained.

Chapter 11 delves into morale, deception, and psychological manipulation, arguing that it is crucial to force your own soldiers to fight with desperation while causing the enemy to fight with doubt.

This book is an excellent dive into the mind of one of history's greatest military strategists. It provided me with a fresh perspective on the nuances of "strategy" and "tactics," while shedding light on the critical elements that anchor them, such as supply chains and environmental awareness. I highly recommend it.
